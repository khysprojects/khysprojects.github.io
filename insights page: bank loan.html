Bank Loan Insights

This project analyzes loan application data to understand the factors involved in loan approval decisions. It looks at variables such as credit score, debt-to-income ratio, loan amount, and applicant characteristics to explore how applications differ across the dataset and what lenders take into account when assessing them.

Credit Risk and Affordability

Key Insights

  • Strong negative relationship: As debt-to-income (DTI) goes up, approval rates drop pretty quickly. It’s one of the strongest trends in the data.
  • High approval “safe zone”: between about 0.05 and 0.30, where approval rates stay relatively high (around 60–75%) and don’t change much.
  • Tipping point around 0.35–0.40: After this point, approvals begin to fall off more noticeably, which suggests a likely cutoff used in decision-making.
  • Steep drop beyond 0.45: Once DTI exceeds 0.45, approval rates fall significantly (~20%–40%) and become more volatile.
  • Zone 0.50 is highly risky : At DTI above 0.50, approval rates drop to 10%–25%, indicating that applications in this range are rarely approved.
  • Low DTI variability: At very low DTI levels (below 0.05), approval rates vary more. This suggests that other factors, such as credit score or income, may have a stronger influence in these cases.
  • Non-linear relationship: The decline is not gradual, t shows a threshold effect, meaning lenders treat DTI as a cutoff-based rule, not just a continuous factor.

Key Insights

  • Clear approval split: Approved applications cluster around higher average credit scores and lower average debt-to-income (DTI), while rejected applications cluster around lower scores and higher DTI.
  • DTI looks like a major rejection trigger: Rejected groups sit roughly in the 0.31–0.41 DTI range, versus approved groups around 0.22–0.25, suggesting lenders are strongly penalizing heavier debt burden.
  • Credit score rises with age: Older age bands generally show higher average credit scores, which suggests longer credit history and stronger repayment profiles.
  • Older applicants also carry larger financial exposure: The larger bubbles for 45–54 and 55+ age groups show they tend to request bigger loan amounts.
  • Higher score alone does not guarantee approval: Some older groups have stronger average credit scores, but when DTI is high, they still appear in the rejected cluster. This suggests the decision is based on a combination of score and affordability, not score alone.
  • Younger applicants appear weaker on both dimensions: The Under 25 and 25–34 age groups sit at lower average scores and, in the rejected cluster, relatively elevated DTI, making them more vulnerable to denial.
  • Approved older applicants still keep DTI controlled: Even when approved applicants are older and ask for larger loans, their average DTI stays relatively low. That suggests affordability discipline is key.

Employement Factors

Key Insights

  • Applicant mix is heavily skewed toward employed individuals: Around 70% of applicants are employed, compared to ~20% self-employed and ~10% students. This means most overall approval trends and portfolio performance are driven by the employed segment, making it the core customer base.
  • Students have the highest approval rates for credit cards: Students outperform other groups in Credit Card approvals (~75%). This suggests lenders treat credit cards as a low-risk, entry-level product for individuals with limited credit history.
  • Students struggle with larger or longer-term products: Approval rates drop significantly for Personal Loans and Lines of Credit among students. This indicates that lenders perceive students as higher risk for larger financial commitments, likely due to lower income and shorter credit history.
  • Self-employed applicants show mixed and less predictable outcomes: Approval rates are moderate across all products, without clear dominance in any category. This likely reflects income variability and less stable financial profiles, which increases uncertainty in lending decisions.
  • Employed applicants are the most stable and consistent segment: Approval rates are relatively even across all product types (~55–65%). This consistency suggests that steady employment provides reliable income signals, making this group the most predictable for lenders.
  • Clear product risk hierarchy across all segments: Credit Cards → highest approval rates Lines of Credit → moderate Personal Loans → lowest approval rates

Approval Drivers: Credit Score & Loan Burden

Key Insights

  • Approval rate strongly increases with credit score: Clear upward trend from Poor (25%) → Fair (55%) → Good (75%) → Very Good (85%) → Excellent (90%)
  • Big jump between Poor and Fair segments: Moving from Poor to Fair nearly doubles approval probability which Suggests a minimum threshold effect where very low scores are heavily penalized
  • Diminishing gains at higher scores: Improvement from Very Good to Excellent is relatively small. This Indicates lenders may treat high-score applicants similarly once a “safe” level is reached.

Key Insights

  • Most applicants fall in a moderate range: The majority are concentrated between ~0.1 and 0.6 loan-to-income ratio, Indicating most borrowers request loans that are reasonable relative to their income.
  • Peak concentration around mid-low ratios: Highest bars appear roughly between 0.2 and 0.5 which Suggests this is the typical borrowing behaviour in your dataset.
  • Gradual decline as ratio increases: As loan-to-income rises beyond 0.6–0.8, the number of applicants steadily decreases.
  • Sharp drop-off in high-risk ranges: Very few applicants above 1.2–1.5 which Indicates fewer people attempt (or qualify for) high leverage loans.
  • Long right tail (high-risk segment exists):Some applicants reach ratios close to 2.0, Representing a small but potentially risky group
